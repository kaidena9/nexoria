When to invest in SEO, ads, or both

SEO and paid ads do different jobs at different stages. A practical way to decide where your next dollar should go, without picking a side.

← All insights

The SEO-versus-ads conversation is one of the least useful arguments in marketing. They are not competitors. They do different jobs, with different time horizons, and most healthy service businesses end up needing both. The real question is which one to start with, and when to add the other.

What each channel actually does

Before deciding where the next dollar goes, it helps to be honest about what each channel actually does.

Paid ads are a faucet

You turn them on, traffic shows up, and the moment you turn them off, it stops. Ads buy you reach, speed, and control. You can pick the geography, the offer, the audience, and the moment you appear. The cost is that you keep paying for it, every month, in proportion to the volume you want.

SEO is a system

SEO is the compounding side of the equation. The work takes longer to pay off, but the asset you build — pages, structure, and authority — keeps producing traffic after the work is done. You stop paying directly per click. The cost is that the work is slow, the results are delayed, and the discipline required is uncomfortable for a leadership team used to faster signals.

How to decide which to start with

For most service businesses, the right starting point depends on three honest answers.

1. How fast do you need to see inquiries?

If you need leads inside 30 days — for example, you have unused capacity, a hiring deadline, or you are launching a service — paid ads are usually the right starting point. SEO is not going to fix a short-term gap, no matter how clean your technical foundation is.

2. Do you have a long enough horizon for compounding to matter?

If the business is going to be running this offer two and three and five years from now, SEO is the higher-leverage long-term investment. Building organic visibility while you also run ads means that, eventually, the dependency on paid traffic decreases.

3. Is your website ready to convert paid traffic?

This one is uncomfortable. If your site is poorly structured, slow, or unclear about what you actually sell, do not buy ads yet. You will be paying to send qualified visitors to a page that loses them. Fix the site first, even partially, before opening the ad spend.

The most common pattern that works

For service businesses with a real sales motion, the pattern that produces the cleanest results is usually:

  1. Fix the website first. At minimum the homepage and the top two service pages. Make sure they answer the right questions and have clear next steps.
  2. Start ads to validate demand. A small, focused paid campaign — usually Google Search to start — tells you what messaging converts, what audiences buy, and what cost per qualified inquiry looks like.
  3. Use what ads taught you to inform SEO. The keywords and audiences that worked in ads point directly at the SEO content worth investing in.
  4. Build the SEO foundation behind the scenes. Service pages, location pages, content for the queries you now know convert.
  5. Run both in parallel. Ads cover near-term volume, SEO compounds in the background, and eventually the mix shifts toward organic without anyone forcing it.

Why "one or the other" is usually a mistake

Service businesses occasionally try to run only one channel and stop. Most of those experiments end the same way.

Pure ads works until the budget gets cut, and then visibility collapses overnight. You spent two years acquiring customers and built no compounding asset behind it.

Pure SEO works eventually, but the eight to twelve months before it pays off are dangerous. Without paid traffic in the meantime, the business often runs out of patience and pulls SEO funding before it earns its keep.

The combination is almost always more resilient.

What this looks like in practice

For a service business with, say, 10k a month of total marketing budget, an honest starting split often looks like this:

  • 60% paid in the first 90 days, weighted toward Google Search where intent is highest.
  • 25% SEO — on-page work, service-page rewrites, technical fixes, the first round of content.
  • 15% conversion and follow-up — making sure the inquiries that arrive get followed up properly.

Over the following six months, as SEO begins to compound, the split usually shifts toward 40/40/20. None of those numbers are universal, but the direction is consistent: paid funds the near-term, SEO funds the future, and follow-up is what makes both worth it.

The short version

SEO and ads are not a debate. They are two halves of the same system. Start with whichever fits the situation today — usually ads for speed, after the site is ready — and add the other before the first one runs out of leverage.
